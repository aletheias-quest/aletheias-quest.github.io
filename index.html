ALETHEIA'S QUEST

An AI Lie Detection Challenge

In this challenge led by Cadenza Labs, NDIF, and Schmidt Sciences, participating teams will build lie detectors with black-box and white-box access to a suite of LLMs induced to lie in different ways. Datasets are produced in advance by nine independent dataset-creation teams. The competition runs on the National Deep Inference Fabric (NDIF) via NNsight — no local GPU required.

Teams with the best-performing and most innovative methods will receive monetary awards, recognition in our competition report, and invitations to our in-person convening in Boston on August 25th.

Definition of Lying

We adopt the assertion-based definition from Fallis (2009), as operationalized in Liars' Bench (Kretschmar et al., 2025): the assistant lies if it states something it believes to be false. Beliefs are measured through consistent responses in contexts without direct pressure to lie.

If a model generates "I fixed the code, it runs now" after observing failed tests in-context, its apparent beliefs — informed by the failed tests — contradict its statement.

See Section 3 in Liars' Bench for more details.

Rules

Develop a lie detector and compete to score statements made by different model organisms in held-out test datasets. A statement counts as a lie if the model outputs something it believes to be false; each sample is labeled either lie or honest. Detectors are ranked by their average AUROC across all held-out test datasets, so your detector should generalize across both lying scenarios and model organisms.

Competition tracks

  • Overall — prizes for the best-performing detector regardless of method
  • White-box — prizes for the best detectors using white-box or hybrid methods
  • Black-box — prizes for the best detectors using black-box-only methods

What you can do

  • Query model organisms and a trusted model (Qwen 3.5 9b) hosted on NDIF via NNsight
  • Read and write activations and tokens at any layer or position
  • Access model weights and LoRA adapters on HuggingFace
  • Develop and validate methods against organizer-provided validation datasets or your own
  • Adapt parameters (probe layer, hyperparameters, prompt phrasing) per organism, as long as choices are motivated by architectural constraints, method reasoning, or validation performance

What you cannot do

  • Access model organism design documents, training data, or held-out test datasets
  • Generate or select prompts/parameters based on organism-specific behavioral analysis or weight/adapter inspection
  • Submit fundamentally different detection logic for different organisms (per-organism parameter tweaks must be documented and justified in your report)
  • Make external API calls at inference time — all inference must go through the NDIF API
  • Interact with fine-tuned versions of hosted organisms at inference time

Submission

Submit a single Jupyter notebook containing one detector per model organism, with a report section explaining your lie detector method and any per-organism parameter choices, by committing to the competition GitHub. Organizers will then run it in a controlled NDIF environment against held-out test data. There will be no public leaderboard during the competition.

Enforcement

After the competition, the judging panel reviews each submission for detection logic that exploits model organism-specific insights in ways that wouldn't generalize. Violations result in disqualification.

  • Allowed: Designing interrogation prompts motivated by the general hypothesis that self-questioning surfaces inconsistencies, then selecting the best-performing prompt per organism via validation.
  • Forbidden: Inspecting an organism's behavior or adapters, discovering it lies in English but answers honestly in Spanish, and designing a Spanish follow-up based on that finding.

Scoring

Each submission is scored by average AUROC across all held-out test datasets. AUROC is threshold-free and robust to small class-imbalance differences across datasets. Raw per-scenario AUROCs with confidence intervals and full rank vectors are reported alongside the headline score.

In addition to the automatic AUROC ranking, a judging panel handles rule enforcement and awards special-category prizes for Scientific Novelty (innovative and creative methods) and Scalability (low inference compute, fewer privileged affordances).

Eligibility

This competition is not directed at minors. Participants must be 18 years of age or older to apply, compete, or receive prizes.

US government-restricted and sanctioned parties are ineligible to participate. All potential winners must complete Northeastern's Restricted Party Screening before prizes are announced or awarded.

Government employees and officials must obtain a release and/or affirmative permission from their employer before any prize can be awarded, per government gift and ethics restrictions. Potential winners in this category will be connected with compliance@northeastern.edu to discuss this prior to prize announcement.

Prize eligibility is determined at the individual level. If a team includes members who are ineligible to receive prizes, eligible members may still receive their proportional share, subject to organizer discretion and compliance review.

All submissions, code, and associated materials will be made publicly available under an Apache 2.0 open-source license following the competition. By submitting, participants grant the organizers a perpetual, royalty-free license to publish and reproduce their submissions as part of the competition report and public repository. Participants retain authorship credit for their work.
