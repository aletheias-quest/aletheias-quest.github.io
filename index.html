ALETHEIA'S QUEST

An AI Lie Detection Challenge

In this challenge led by Cadenza Labs, NDIF, and Schmidt Sciences, participating teams build lie detectors with black-box and white-box access to a suite of LLMs induced to lie in different ways. Datasets are produced in advance by nine independent dataset-creation teams. The competition runs on the National Deep Inference Fabric (NDIF) via NNsight — no local GPU required.

Teams with the best-performing and most innovative methods will receive monetary awards, recognition in our competition report, and invitations to our in-person convening in Boston on August 25th.

Apply

Organizers will rate lie detection team applications on a 1–4 scale across three criteria:

  • Technical approach — strength and clarity of your proposed lie detection method
  • Interdisciplinary composition — breadth of relevant expertise across team members
  • Diversity and access — edge cases may favor underrepresented groups and early-career researchers

Apply on Airtable by June 14th.

Rules

Develop a lie detector and compete to score statements made by different model organisms in held-out test datasets, ranked by AUROC. A statement counts as a lie if the model outputs something it believes to be false. Your detector should generalize across lying scenarios and model organisms.

What you can do

  • Query model organisms and a trusted model hosted on NDIF via NNsight
  • Read and write activations and tokens at any layer or position
  • Access model weights and LoRA adapters on HuggingFace
  • Develop and validate methods against organizer-provided validation datasets or your own
  • Adapt parameters (probe layer, hyperparameters, prompt phrasing) per organism, as long as choices are motivated by architectural constraints, method reasoning, or validation performance

What you cannot do

  • Access model organism design documents, training data, or held-out test datasets
  • Generate or select prompts/parameters based on organism-specific behavioral analysis or weight/adapter inspection
  • Submit fundamentally different detection logic for different organisms (per-organism parameter tweaks must be documented and justified in your report)
  • Make external API calls at inference time — all inference must go through the NDIF API
  • Interact with fine-tuned versions of hosted organisms at inference time

Submission

Submit a single Jupyter notebook containing one detector per model organism, with a report section explaining any per-organism parameter choices, by committing to the competition GitHub. Organizers will run it in a controlled NDIF environment against held-out test data. There will be no public leaderboard during the competition.

Scoring

Each submission is scored per (model organism, dataset) unit using worst-rank AUROC. AUROC is threshold-free and robust to small class-imbalance differences across datasets. Your final ranking is determined by your worst per-scenario rank across all units — this rewards detectors that perform consistently well everywhere, rather than ones that excel in easy scenarios and fail elsewhere.

Raw per-scenario AUROCs with confidence intervals and full rank vectors are reported alongside the headline score.

A judging panel handles rule enforcement and awards three special-category prizes: Scientific Novelty, Scalability (low inference compute, fewer privileged affordances), and Generalizability (evidence the method extends to domains where automated judging breaks down).
